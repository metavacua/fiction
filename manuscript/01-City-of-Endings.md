# City of Endings

The narrative city is less a place than a process. It accrues stories in layers, striated like the walls of a disused quarry, sedimentary and inescapable. When I arrive, newly hired at the Institute and hungover from the caffeine crash of my over-caffeinated night, the city is half-finished in the way all living things are: still mutating, still adjusting its features to whatever its residents need it to be.

My assigned quarters are in a converted memory-archive, a former library gutted and reborn as transient housing for postdocs and visiting researchers. The ceilings are too high, the rooms echo with every step, and at night, the plumbing shrieks as if mourning its own obsolescence. I take comfort in these flaws—there’s nothing more suspicious than a city that’s finished with itself.

First morning: I find the kitchen by following the aroma of burnt toast and molecular regret. A figure stands by the sink, sipping from a cracked mug, their silhouette backlit by the stained-glass window that depicts, not a saint or martyr, but a diagram of Aristotle’s dramatic arc.

They look up as I enter. “You’re the new quant at the Institute?” they ask, more statement than question.

I nod, too aware of my own presence. “Maya Patel. Here to forcibly extract data from mythological thinking.”

The figure laughs—a bright, fissile sound. “You’re a little late for iconoclasm. The city runs on myth.”

“I thought that was the problem.”

They smile, approach, and extend a hand. “Morgan Aldrich. Department of Narrative Studies. We used to have an actual handshake here, but now we’re required to improvise our own greetings.”

I take their hand, unsure what to do with it. “What did people do before?”

“Usually just left without saying goodbye.” Morgan releases my hand, their grip lingering like a secret handshake. “Coffee?”

I decline, already vibrating at a frequency best measured in hertz. “I have to report to the director at nine.”

Morgan glances at the wall clock, which displays time not as numbers but as a looping segment of film, spliced with blank frames that represent the future. “You’ve got thirty-seven minutes. If you want, I’ll walk you.”

I want to say, "That won’t be necessary," but the words abort themselves. Instead, I find myself trailing Morgan through the building’s corridors, each lined with the relics of failed organizational paradigms: open-plan offices abandoned after the Group Narrative Catastrophe, cubicles lined with acoustic foam, seminar rooms partitioned by rolling whiteboards etched with ancient equations.

We step out into the city, and the air is electric. The sky is overcast, the light diffuse, as if the city is determined not to cast any shadow deeper than the ones you bring with you. The sidewalks shimmer with freshly sown narrative seeds, little pips of story designed to take root in passing minds. In the plaza, a woman in a purple coat sits on a bench, feeding not pigeons, but origami birds that refold themselves after every bite.

Morgan gestures toward the sight. “Do you know her?”

I shake my head.

“You will,” they say, and for a moment I almost believe it.

The Institute’s main building looks nothing like the renderings on its website. The exterior is an optical illusion, designed to appear different from every angle. From the west, it’s a fortress; from the east, a cathedral; from above, a question mark. Inside, the director’s office is lined with trophies—fossilized fragments of old worldviews, preserved in lucite. The director herself is a relic: hair styled in the fashion of a century ago, lipstick the color of arterial blood.

She motions for me to sit. Morgan hovers at the threshold, half-shadow.

“We’re glad to have you,” the director says, in a tone that suggests the opposite. “Your reputation precedes you, as does your pattern.”

I tilt my head, deflecting with a question. “Which pattern is that?”

“You never stay. You disrupt, you innovate, and then you’re gone.”

I let the accusation hang. In this city, they’ve already written me into the meta-story, and there’s no point pretending otherwise.

“That’s my training,” I say. “And my function.”

“Let’s hope you fulfill both,” the director says. She stands, turns her back, and starts rearranging the artifacts on her shelves. It’s a dismissal, but I linger a moment, as if waiting for a cue.

Morgan meets me outside, hands in pockets, eyes not quite on mine. “So. How did it go?”

I shrug. “She expects me to fail, but in a productive way.”

Morgan nods, as if this was always the point.

We drift through the city, half in conversation, half not. Morgan points out landmarks: the Epitaph Fountain, whose jets spell out different names at random; the Contradiction Steps, each riser cut at a different angle so you never know how high your foot will land; the Market of Borrowed Scenes, where vendors hawk not goods, but snippets of other people’s memories.

Morgan buys me one: a fleeting vision of a childhood picnic, sunlight on watermelon, someone’s voice calling from far off. “To get you acclimated,” they say. “Everyone needs at least one fake memory to belong.”

I want to tell them I have plenty, all real, none helpful. Instead, I let the memory dissolve on my tongue, sweet and unfamiliar.

In the days that follow, the city begins its slow digestion. I am assigned an office—windowless, damp—and set to work analyzing the narrative flow of municipal ordinances. The Institute is obsessed with the problem of closure: how to ensure that every act, every transaction, has a proper ending. Half the city’s GDP is spent on elaborate rituals to guarantee narrative satisfaction: reconciliation dinners, public apologies, annual burnings of obsolete legislation.

I find it exhausting. I prefer my stories ragged at the edges, open to revision. Every time the city tries to shoehorn a life into a pre-fab narrative arc, I flinch.

Morgan becomes my default company. We lunch in the courtyard, or walk the bridge over the canal, or sit on the Institute’s roof, sharing cigarettes we don’t smoke, just burn for the ceremony of it. Our conversations are fractal: stories inside stories, each with its own punchline or moral, but always looping back to the original question of whether any of it matters.

One night, after too much wine and too little inhibition, Morgan asks, “Have you ever wanted to stay, just once?”

I laugh, surprised at the bluntness. “It’s not about wanting. It’s about what happens if I do.”

“Which is?”

I study my hands, embarrassed by their transparency. “I’m not sure. I think I’d disappear. Or worse, I’d calcify—become someone so fixed in their role that even I wouldn’t recognize myself.”

Morgan leans in, voice low. “I don’t think that’s true.”

“Then you don’t know me,” I say, more sharply than intended.

Morgan holds my gaze, unblinking. “Maybe I’d like to.”

Later, in my room, I replay the conversation, dissecting every possible reading, every alternate ending. I feel a tension in my chest—not emotional, but physical, as if my ribs are bracing for impact. I get up, pace the length of the room, touch every book and object as if anchoring myself in the world.

At some point, I strip off my clothes and stand before the window, illuminated by the city’s somber glow. My body is ordinary: a sum of parts, some functional, some disappointing, all arranged in a configuration I’ve never quite managed to claim as my own. I trace the outline of my shoulder, wondering if it would look different in someone else’s story.

A sudden itch on my forearm—sharp, urgent. I scratch, and a flake of skin comes away, revealing a lattice of text just beneath the surface, words and sentences that seem to shimmer with each new pulse of my heart. I blink, rub my eyes, and the illusion is gone, but the sensation lingers: as if my body is being overwritten, line by line, by a story I did not consent to.

I collapse into bed, heart pounding. I want to call Morgan, but I don’t. Instead, I write a letter—to myself, to the version of me that might exist if I ever manage to stay. The words are clumsy, full of apologies and impossible hopes. I fold it, seal it, and slip it under my pillow, where it can’t betray me.

The next day, the city resumes its work of assimilation. Morgan meets me at the corner cafe, already halfway through their first espresso.

“Rough night?” they ask.

“Too many stories,” I say, and mean it.

They reach out, thumb a strand of hair from my brow. The gesture is gentle, deliberate. I feel the urge to flinch, but don’t. Instead, I let the contact linger, let the moment pass without commentary.

Morgan leans back, studies me. “You’re different today.”

“Am I?”

“A little. Like you’re waiting for something.”

I want to say, "I’m always waiting," but instead I just smile, weakly.

We walk. The city is abuzz with a festival—The Iteration Parade, in which citizens dress up as alternate versions of themselves, each more absurd than the last. A man in a bowler hat argues with a version of himself in a pirate costume; a child dressed as a sentient cloud floats helium balloons to mimic her chosen form. Morgan and I watch from a bridge, trading commentary.

“Which version would you be?” Morgan asks.

“I’d probably just be me, but with better hair,” I say.

Morgan shakes their head. “That’s a cop-out.”

“Is it?”

“I think you’d be exactly who you are, only more so.”

I consider this, then nod. “Maybe you’re right.”

We linger at the bridge, watching the crowd. Below, the water flows dark and constant, indifferent to whatever stories people impose on its surface.

In the quiet that follows, Morgan turns to me, and for a moment, I see every possible version of myself reflected in their eyes: the leaver, the stayer, the guest, the host. It is terrifying, and also the first time I’ve felt truly seen.

Later that week, a crisis: the director calls me in, demands a report on the narrative instability spreading through the city. “People are improvising their endings,” she says, voice tight with alarm. “Abandoning the structures we’ve worked so hard to build. We can’t let that happen.”

I want to tell her it’s inevitable—that every system, given time, produces its own subversion. But I hold my tongue, instead drafting a memorandum so full of jargon and ambiguity it can serve as its own denial.

Afterward, I find Morgan on the Institute roof, staring at the skyline.

“Did you ever think,” I ask, “that the city’s problem isn’t its lack of endings, but its obsession with having the right one?”

Morgan laughs, a quick, bright pulse of sound. “You sound like someone who wants to burn the place down.”

“Not burn,” I say. “Just loosen the screws a little.”

Morgan tilts their head, regarding me. “What if you started with yourself?”

The question lands heavy. I turn it over, searching for a safe angle.

“I don’t know how,” I admit.

Morgan puts their arm around me, the gesture as light as breath. “Maybe just let the story happen. Even if you don’t know the ending.”

I nod, the words catching in my throat. For the first time, I want to try.

We sit together as the sun sets, the city casting long, improbable shadows across the rooftops. I reach for Morgan’s hand, and this time, I don’t let go.

That night, I write another letter—this one addressed to Morgan, though I don’t know if I’ll ever deliver it. It begins, “I think I want to stay,” and ends with a line I’ve never allowed myself: “You make it possible to imagine a different story.”

I seal the letter, and instead of hiding it, I leave it on the table. In the morning, Morgan finds it, smiles, and pours me coffee as if nothing has changed, though everything has.

The city, ever-responsive, seems to relax its grip, just a fraction. I see it in the slant of the sunlight, the unprogrammed laughter of children in the plaza, the way even the most rigid routines begin to fray at the edges.

I walk to work, no longer mapping every possible exit, no longer planning the next escape. For the first time, I let myself exist in the present tense, uncertain but unafraid.

And I wonder, as I pass the Placeholder statue on my way to the Institute, whether she ever expected her own story to be written anew each day. Whether, like me, she eventually learned to savor the unfinishedness of things.

I think she did. I think, maybe, we all do.

At least, that’s the version I’m telling myself. For now.

The narrative theorists at the Institute have a term for it: "midpoint reversal." A structural inevitability wherein the protagonist, having comfortably nestled into the arc they thought was theirs, discovers a plot twist designed specifically to upend them. The Institute employs dozens of scholars to diagram such reversals, track their frequency across epochs, and publish incomprehensible treatises with names like "Counterfactual Topographies of the Subjunctive Self."

I read these reports in the evenings, when the city is quiet and the streets below my window are lacquered with the yellowed light of sodium lamps. Morgan often sits at the foot of my bed, one leg curled, reading aloud the most absurd phrases they can find, dissecting the arguments with gentle derision. They never read the same page twice. Their memory for words is exact; they could recite the entire bibliography if asked.

Tonight, they choose a passage about the Parable of the Perpetual Guest—some ancient traveler cursed to attend every going-away party, but always departing before dessert is served.

“Doesn’t that sound like you?” Morgan asks, slanting their face toward me.

I consider this. “If it does, it’s only because you’re the host in every scenario.”

Morgan cocks an eyebrow. “And yet, in all versions, the guest leaves.”

“Not always,” I counter. “In some, the guest becomes the party.”

They smile, as if they see the shape of my logic before I do. “You’re doing it again.”

“What?”

“Deflecting. Hiding inside a recursive anecdote.” They rise, cross the room, and perch on the windowsill, the city’s glow backlighting them. “If you don’t want to talk about it, you can just say so.”

“I don’t want to talk about it,” I say, but the words feel underdressed, exposed in the cool air between us.

Morgan accepts this, returning to their book. The room is silent except for the shuffle of pages. I turn inward, as always, processing the day's minor traumas: the thinly-veiled threat in my advisor’s memo, the awkward exchange with a neighbor at the mail kiosk, the way the barista at the corner shop started calling me by name without permission.

I am a collector of small discomforts. Every evening, I line them up in a mental diorama, arrange and rearrange them, until the tableau feels almost manageable. On better nights, Morgan will reach into my thoughts and pull one out, examine it under the lamp, and laugh until we both do.

Tonight, though, the city’s narrative machinery grinds just below the threshold of perception, and I am acutely aware of my own subplot.

“I’m thinking about writing another letter,” I say.

“To whom?”

“You.”

Morgan closes the book. “Why a letter, when you can just talk to me?”

“It’s easier to be honest in text,” I say, and it’s not even a lie.

Morgan nods, silent. They do not challenge the premise.

I try again. “What would you say if I left?”

They do not answer right away. The silence lengthens, becomes something tactile, a fabric pulled taut between us.

Finally, Morgan says, “I would say that I wish you didn’t feel you had to.”

“Is that all?”

“No.” They look at me, and their gray eyes are enormous, moons caught in a tide. “I would say that I love you. But I’d wait until you were gone, so you’d have to replay it in your mind for the rest of your life.”

The line lands with a force that surprises even them. They blink, swallow. I imagine the world’s tiniest fault line opening in the floorboards, preparing for the next seismic shift.

I have never understood how to respond to declarations like that. The only way I know to return such feelings is to dismantle them, test their tensile strength, see if the words hold up under analysis. But I have already written two letters to Morgan in the last month, and neither made it out of the folio.

Instead, I say, “If I left, it wouldn’t be because of you. You know that.”

“I know,” Morgan says, so quietly it barely qualifies as speech. “But you would make it about me, in the letter.”

It’s true. The draft I wrote yesterday begins, "I’m sorry for not loving you better," and ends with a footnote explaining the evolutionary logic of emotional self-sabotage.

Morgan’s face is unreadable in the window’s backlight. For the first time, I wonder if I am the narrative parasite the Institute’s legends warn about—feeding off connection until I can metabolize the nutrients, then moving on to the next host.

I stand, move to the window, and press my shoulder to theirs. “Are you hungry?” I ask, because I can’t hold the tension any longer.

“Starving,” Morgan says. “Let’s go somewhere expensive and order everything we can’t pronounce.”

We do. We walk the spiraling streets to a restaurant that serves food arranged in the shapes of punctuation marks: commas of pickled radish, exclamation points of octopus tentacle, ellipses of custard dusted with edible graphite. We eat and drink, and Morgan narrates the interior lives of every diner in the room—who will stay, who will leave, who will one day regret neither. I study their hands as they gesture; I think about the letter I will write, and wonder if I can ever capture the exact way their knuckles flex when they mimic my voice.

After dinner, we wander. The city hums with narrative intent, and everywhere, people are staging their own stories: a couple arguing, their shadows crossing and uncrossing on the pavement; a street musician playing an instrument made from discarded water pipes; a pair of teenagers documenting each other’s tears with a camera, as if auditioning for the best tragedy in the district.

Morgan turns to me on the steps of a monument—this one, a bronze archway with footnotes inscribed along the base, each commemorating a notable argument that occurred at its threshold. “Do you ever wish we were different?” they ask.

“Different how?”

“Less aware. More capable of just… being. Without always waiting for the next reversal.”

I know what they mean, but I can’t help myself. “If we were less aware, we wouldn’t have found each other.”

Morgan snorts. “You always win the arguments where you set the terms.”

I shrug. “That’s how logic works.”

We lean against the arch, feeling the collective gravity of every breakup, every reconciliation that has ever transpired here. The city is littered with places like this—sites where story and memory fuse, where even strangers sense the residue of heightened feeling.

Morgan sighs, a full-body gesture. “I want to take you somewhere.”

“Now?”

“Now.” Their hand closes around mine, and I let myself be led.

We walk, wordlessly, through an unfamiliar sector. The architecture grows stranger: windows set at odd angles, doors built to open only outward, pedestrian bridges that dead-end into nothing but air. At last, we arrive at a building with no sign, just a sequence of etched numbers above the door: 4.7.2.1.

Inside, the ceiling arches impossibly high. The walls are lined with mirrors, none of which reflect the actual occupants of the room. Instead, each mirror displays a different scene: a woman sitting alone in a coffee shop, a group of friends clinking glasses, a small child asleep in a car’s backseat. I recognize none of them, and all of them.

“It’s a memory palace,” Morgan explains. “But not for individuals. It’s collective. Community. Every person who’s ever lived here, their most significant moment is stored somewhere in these walls.”

I am momentarily dizzy. “Why bring me here?”

Morgan takes a slow breath. “Because your name is already in the registry.”

We step deeper. A directory screen flickers on near the center, and Morgan types “Maya Patel.” Instantly, hundreds of entries populate—none current, all past, all other iterations, some alive, some not.

I stare at the data, then at Morgan. “How long have you known?”

“Since the day we met,” Morgan says. “The world repeats itself, but always with variations. I wanted to know if this time, you would choose to stay.”

It feels like an accusation, but Morgan’s face is gentle. They reach out, tuck a strand of hair behind my ear.

“I want to show you one more thing,” they say.

We walk to a side chamber, smaller, lined in velvet. Here, the mirrors are blank. Instead, a single pedestal holds an envelope—my handwriting, unmistakable. Dated tomorrow.

I pick it up, fingers trembling. The world seems to fold, then unfold.

“Open it,” Morgan urges.

Inside, a single sentence: “This time, I left so I could come back.”

I look up at Morgan, searching for duplicity, for mischief. Instead, I find hope, unguarded and impossible.

“I don’t understand,” I say, but already I do.

Morgan’s arms go around me, and for a moment, I allow it. The intimacy is not sudden, nor is it new; it is the gradual closing of a circuit, a realization that even departures are a way of anchoring oneself to something else.

We stand, holding each other, as the city’s narrative engine hums softly beneath our feet, spinning out new versions, new possible returns.

Later, back in my apartment, I start the letter again. This time, I write without editing, without footnotes, without an escape clause. I fold it, seal it, and place it on the windowsill.

I don’t know if I will leave, or stay, or leave and return. But for the first time, I am not afraid of the midpoint reversal. For the first time, I allow myself to want the second half of the story.

In the morning, Morgan wakes me with coffee, their hand on my waist, their lips against the back of my neck. We don’t talk about the letter, but it is there, waiting, just in case.

We sip our coffee in the growing light, side by side, neither of us pretending that the ending will be happy, but also not pretending that it must be anything else.

For a little while, we just exist, perfectly poised at the center of our own narrative, holding the reversal at bay.

# Scene 1 - from Maya Patel's point of view

The city is made for endings. From above, the departure points gleam—spiral terraces, slotted between glass towers like bone joints, always open to the air, always with just enough wind to flick hair across your face, obscure your expression, keep you from being fully known.

I arrive early. The sun hasn't shed its skin yet, and the spaces between buildings are colorless, all anticipation. I cross the plaza, passing a statue of a woman whose hands lift her own head above her shoulders, her features slightly out of focus. Her name, if she has one, has already worn away; people just call her The Placeholder.

I like The Placeholder. I used to bring my lunch here, sitting under the shade of her impossible arms. She reminds me of my mother, and the logic of that comparison is obvious and disquieting. Mothers make you, but only ever so incompletely that you're forced to spend the rest of your life in iterative approximation, trying to fit some missing piece into your own outline.

I have never fit, but I have always approximated. Today's approximation: navy trousers, linen shirt (creased from the folds, I do not iron), a scarf the color of bruised fruit at my neck, and a leather folio under my arm. Inside: three letters, all addressed to Morgan Aldrich. One for now, one for when I'm gone, and one for a version of me I am not yet.

At precisely seven minutes past the hour, Morgan rounds the corner. They are incapable of being late, but never arrive early. Punctuality as an art form, a manner of keeping all outcomes equidistant from their own desire. Morgan walks with the economy of a practiced performer, hands in the pockets of their coat, lips parted in the process of a sentence I haven't heard yet.

"You're here," they say, as if they've conjured me.

"As are you," I counter, the echo intentional. We are always playacting, always conducting an orchestra only we can hear.

Morgan sits beside me on the bench. Their profile is flawless in the half-light, but only if you like faces that can change shape with a single shift in angle or intent. They have a gift for transformation, not in the literal sense—I have seen their passport photo, perfectly unremarkable—but in the way of those who can become necessary, then vanish, without leaving anything you could take to a police sketch artist.

They scan my face, reading, as always, for subtext.

"So. This is the ending," Morgan says. "Are you ready?"

"Insofar as anyone is ready for a narrative collapse. I brought props." I tap the folio. "Do you want the letters now, or later?"

"Always later," Morgan says, which is how I know they are stalling. In our version of the world, 'later' means 'not at all.'

We sit, then, in the hush. The city feeds endings to its citizens like communion. Somewhere, a bell rings: it is not the hour, but the signal that the last train has left the district. It is a courtesy. No one wants to be stranded in the interstitials if they don't mean to be.

"You could stay," Morgan says. "They have new positions at the Institute. You'd have your own office. People would come to you."

"People always come to me," I say, with more acid than I intend. "But that's not how the story goes. We make a change, we measure the result, and then we walk away. Otherwise we become the data."

Morgan laughs—not a real laugh, but the sort that starts at the back of the throat and stalls, waiting for permission to continue.

"What if I want to be the data?" they ask, and it's a little too naked, even for this hour, even for us.

"If you wanted that, you'd have to let me write you down," I say. "The whole of you, not just the parts you curate. Could you handle that?"

Morgan considers. "I could. But could you?"

I don't answer, and in not answering, I answer.

They stand, brush lint from their trousers. "There's still time for coffee. Do you want to?"

I hesitate. There is always time for coffee, just as there is always time for one more conversation, one more farewell refracted through ritual. But the longer you drag it out, the more it becomes the whole story, instead of just the epilogue.

"Let's walk," I say instead. "The boundary is better when approached at speed."

We set off, the clack of our shoes counting out the seconds of this shared fiction. The streets are empty, except for a child drawing concentric circles in the dust with a stick, her tongue poking from the corner of her mouth in deep concentration. When she looks up, her gaze slides right through us; in the logic of this place, we are already gone.

"You never told me your real reason for leaving," Morgan says. "Was it me?"

"Isn't it always?" I say, but I mean it kindly.

Morgan presses. "No, seriously. Was it?"

I try to explain in the only language I have. "I can't love something unless I'm already in mourning for it. If I stay, I lose the shape of the loss. I become unmoored."

Morgan considers this, and then: "That's incredibly sad."

"It's functional," I correct. "And in my case, evolutionary."

We reach the river. The bridge ahead of us is a relic, built before the era of codified departures, and it sags under the weight of its own memory. A monument at the far side lists every person who has left the city by this route, their names acid-etched into the metal. The names reappear, sometimes decades apart, with small annotations: 'Returned,' 'Stayed,' 'Departed again.'

Morgan slows. I can feel them working up to something, a last plea or a memory to weaponize. Instead, they say, "Can I walk you to the edge?"

"That's the tradition," I say, and though I mean to sound ironic, it lands sincere.

We cross the bridge. Underneath, the water runs fast, indifferent. Morgan's arm is at my elbow, light as a suggestion. I savor the touch, knowing it is a gift with an expiration date.

On the far side, the city boundary is marked not by fences or customs officers, but by a narrative checkpoint: a dais with two chairs, one facing the city, one facing the wilds beyond. A scribe waits at a modest lectern, pen poised above a blank sheet. In this world, all transitions are witnessed, if only by someone paid not to care.

"Do you want to sit?" Morgan asks.

"I'm not the sitting type," I say, but I take the chair anyway. It is surprisingly comfortable, molded to bodies like mine—bodies that have left before, will leave again.

The scribe does not look up, but begins recording, the ink scratching a parallel story to ours.

Morgan stands over me, uncertain. "I don't want this to be the last time."

"It doesn't have to be," I say. "We could always overlap again. Parallel lines intersect, given enough dimensions."

"And will you write?" Morgan says, voice brittle.

"I will always write," I promise, which is both true and irrelevant.

Morgan kneels, takes my hands in theirs. "Then write me good," they say, and I have to bite my lip to keep from breaking.

I want to say something unforgettable. Something that could stand in for every parting, all the ghosts in my luggage. Instead, I press the folio into their hands. "For later," I whisper.

Morgan nods, eyes shining but unspilled. "Safe travels," they say, and the words land with the finality of a performed death.

I stand, shake out my scarf, and step into the boundary. The scribe records my passage with a flourish, as if chronicling the exit of a minor character whose subplot has resolved. I do not look back.

On the other side, the world is the same—trees, sky, the smell of damp earth—but I am altered, ever so slightly. Something about the way the air tugs at my sleeve, or how the horizon now accepts me as part of its equation.

I walk until the city is gone, replaced by the quiet logic of distance. When I finally stop, I allow myself to grieve, not for the leaving, but for the impossibility of ever staying. I open my own letter, the one addressed to me, and read it aloud to the wind. The words are brutal, but I keep reading, because stories only end when you stop telling them.

The wind takes the pages, one by one, and the story continues, neither better nor worse than before—only more itself, and that is enough, for now.
